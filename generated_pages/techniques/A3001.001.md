# Assessment A3001.001: Coercion by Proxy

**Summary**: [Coercion by Proxy](https://freedomhouse.org/report/special-report/2020/home-and-abroad-coercion-proxy-tool-transnational-repression) refers to attempts to control a target through harming or threatening harm to individuals or institutions that the target cares for.<br><br>Instead of attacking a target directly, an actor harms or threatens to harm people the target has a relationship with (family members, colleagues, employers, schools, religious or civic institutions) most often with the goal of coercing, silencing, or punishing the target by proxy.<br><br>Sometimes referred to as “collective punishment”, authoritarian governments operating within their own territory use Coercion by Proxy to target the relatives, associates or institutions connected to critics living or working abroad, using domestic repression as a means of controlling people the state cannot otherwise reach. [Human Rights Watch's global review of how governments repress nationals abroad](https://www.hrw.org/report/2024/02/22/we-will-find-you/global-look-how-governments-repress-nationals-abroad) lists collective punishment of relatives as one of the core recurring methods of transnational repression.<br><br>Coercion by Proxy as a technique is best understood seen through the lens of the chilling effect; the mere possibility of punishment, independent of whether it is ever carried out, is enough to make people restrict their own behaviour out of fear and uncertainty. This matters for Coercion by Proxy specifically because the technique's deterrent power doesn't depend on the original target being harmed at all. A credible threat, or even a single demonstrated case against someone else's family, can chill an entire network of potential sources, journalists or activists who simply witness it.
